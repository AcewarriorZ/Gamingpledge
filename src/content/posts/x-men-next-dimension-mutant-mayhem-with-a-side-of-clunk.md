## “They don’t make ’em like this anymore… and maybe that’s not a bad thing.”

Before superhero games became massive cinematic events, there was a strange little window where licensed comic-book games could be messy, ambitious and oddly charming.

*X-Men: Next Dimension* sits right in that era.

A 3D fighter packed with mutants, dramatic story beats, alternate costumes and enough early-2000s clunk to make you question whether nostalgia is doing some heavy lifting.

## Enter the Danger Room

At its best, *Next Dimension* is a celebration of the X-Men universe.

The roster is genuinely impressive for the time, with a chunky selection of heroes and villains, each bringing their own powers, specials and personality to the fight.

There is also a proper attempt at story, which is more than you can say for a lot of fighting games from this period. It leans into Marvel drama, comic-book stakes and big mutant chaos in a way that gives the game more identity than expected.

It is rough, yes.

But it is not lazy.

## Krakoa cracks begin to show

The problems start when the fighting actually begins.

The combat is stiff, the movement lacks flow and combos never quite feel as smooth as they should. You can see the idea behind the game, but the execution often feels like it is fighting against itself.

The camera does not always help either.

For a game trying to sell dramatic 3D arenas and environmental transitions, it can be surprisingly easy to lose your sense of position mid-fight.

## The good, the bad and the fugly

Visually, *X-Men: Next Dimension* is a product of its time.

Some stages have personality. Some special attacks still have a bit of comic-book flair. But character models and animations can look awkward, especially when compared with stronger fighters from the same era.

Still, there is charm under the grime.

The game clearly wants to be more than a throwaway licensed fighter. It wants to give X-Men fans a full roster, story mode, flashy powers and dramatic mutant battles.

That ambition matters.

## Time travel troubles

Looking back now, *X-Men: Next Dimension* feels like a relic from a very specific gaming era.

It is the kind of game that probably would not get made in the same way today. It is too strange, too specific and too rough around the edges.

But that is also why it is interesting.

It captures a time when licensed games could be flawed, experimental and weirdly full of personality.

## Verdict

*X-Men: Next Dimension* is not a hidden masterpiece.

It is clunky, uneven and visually rough.

But it is also packed with fan service, ambition and that particular PS2-era charm that makes flawed games worth revisiting.

You might not love every fight, but if you grew up with X-Men, Marvel games or early-2000s licensed chaos, there is still something here worth remembering.
