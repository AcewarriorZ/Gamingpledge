# Dead Island 2 Review: A Gloriously Gory Holiday in HELL-A

Dead Island 2 is the kind of sequel you want to love immediately.

Sun-blasted Los Angeles, or HELL-A as the game proudly calls it, has been quarantined. The rich are rotting in their mansions. The streets are packed with undead tourists. And within minutes, you are one bad swing away from turning a swimming pool into a meat blender.

It is flashy, punchy, silly and, when it is firing properly, disgustingly fun.

But once the novelty wears off, and it does, the seams start to show. The story rarely lands. The progression loop does not feel especially rewarding. Exploration is more restricted than it first appears. And the combat pacing can tip from visceral fun into “please stop spawning behind me”.

I am a fan of Dead Island. I wanted this to be the sequel that dragged the series back into the spotlight with confidence.

Instead, Dead Island 2 is a good weekend of co-op chaos rather than a great zombie RPG.

## Quick verdict: is Dead Island 2 worth playing?

Dead Island 2 is worth playing if you want crunchy first-person melee combat, excellent gore and a short, silly zombie campaign to enjoy with friends.

It is harder to recommend if you want a memorable story, deep character progression, rewarding exploration or a sequel that meaningfully evolves the series.

This is not a disaster. Far from it.

It is just not the undead comeback I hoped it would be.

## The hook: when Dead Island 2 is good, it is disgustingly good

Let us get this out of the way.

The melee combat absolutely slaps.

Every swing has weight. Every crunch has consequence. And the gore is not just red paint thrown across the screen for cheap shock value. It is reactive, specific and genuinely horrible in the best possible way.

A blade cuts. A hammer caves. Fire chars. Acid melts. Limbs do not simply fall off because the game needs a flashy effect. They come apart in a way that makes each weapon feel nasty, physical and different.

That is where Dead Island 2 is at its strongest.

There is a brilliant rhythm to the best encounters.

You line up a hit. Bone gives way. A limb goes sightseeing. A zombie stumbles forward with half its face missing. You immediately start planning the next awful thing you are going to do.

It is grim. It is silly. It is very Dead Island.

The game also encourages just enough experimentation to stop combat becoming completely brainless. Dropkicks, flying kicks, explosive barrels, electrified puddles, elemental mods and environmental hazards all feed into that messy playground feeling.

When everything clicks, Dead Island 2 delivers exactly what the series always promised.

Messy, up-close zombie slaughter with style.

## Combat is the main reason to play Dead Island 2

Dead Island 2 lives and dies by its combat, and thankfully, this is the part Dambuster Studios gets mostly right.

The first-person perspective makes every encounter feel personal. You are not floating above the carnage. You are right inside it, backing into walls, stamping on skulls and panic-swinging at anything with teeth.

The weapons are fun too. Pipes, blades, hammers, claws, knives and improvised murder tools all have enough personality to make swapping between them enjoyable.

At its best, Dead Island 2 feels like a zombie brawler with RPG dressing. Not especially deep, but immediately satisfying.

You are not really here for tactical brilliance.

You are here to kick a zombie into a swimming pool, electrify the water and watch the situation become everyone else’s problem.

On that level, it works.

## Skill Cards are a fun idea, but they do not transform the game

Dead Island 2 replaces traditional skill trees with a Skill Card system.

On paper, this sounds like a dream.

You collect cards, slot them into your build, tweak your abilities and adjust your Slayer as you go. It gives the game a lighter, more flexible RPG structure than a fixed upgrade tree.

In practice, it is fine.

Not bad. Not pointless. Just fine.

The system adds flavour, but it rarely changes the moment-to-moment experience as much as I wanted it to. Most cards feel like useful seasoning rather than the main ingredient.

You might tweak your dodge. You might alter your kick. You might lean into certain effects. But I rarely felt like I had crafted a wildly different character.

Fury is the exception.

Fury acts as a proper panic button when the crowd starts closing in, and it gave me some of the game’s best “I should not have survived that” moments.

The Skill Card system is a solid idea.

It just needed more dramatic build variety to fully earn its place.

## Progression and loot: upgrades exist, but the motivation does not

This is where the cracks start spreading.

Dead Island 2 has weapon upgrades, mods, perks and level-matching, but the loot loop never becomes properly compelling.

Yes, you can make your favourite tools nastier.

Yes, it is fun to attach elemental effects and turn a basic weapon into something ridiculous.

But the upgrade flow often pushes you into constant churn.

Find something decent. Mod it. Use it for a bit. Outlevel it. Replace it. Repeat.

That would be fine if the game made replacement feel exciting, but it often feels more practical than thrilling.

And when you do want to keep a beloved weapon relevant, the cost of matching it to your current level can feel steep enough that you simply do not bother.

That is a problem for a game built around loot and upgrades.

A strong loot system should make you hungry for the next discovery. Dead Island 2’s system mostly made me shrug, clear inventory space and move on.

It is not broken.

It is just not sticky enough.

## Exploration: gorgeous places, strangely unrewarding poking about

HELL-A is a fantastic postcard apocalypse.

It is sunny, absurd, glamorous and completely ruined. The setting has personality before anyone even opens their mouth.

There is something instantly appealing about jogging through wrecked celebrity mansions, abandoned hotels and blood-soaked streets while the whole game winks at Los Angeles excess.

Visually, Dead Island 2 has a strong identity.

The problem is that exploration often feels like it is wearing handcuffs.

A lot of areas are blocked off until a specific quest says you are allowed in. Side missions can also be awkwardly gated behind story progress, which makes the world feel less open than it first appears.

Instead of feeling like a dangerous place you are surviving in, HELL-A can feel like a theme park with velvet ropes.

You can see the interesting room.

You just cannot go in yet.

That constant friction hurts the pacing. I wanted to roam, poke around, get distracted and be rewarded for curiosity.

Dead Island 2 lets you explore, but only on its terms.

## Enemy variety is strong, but the balancing can kill the mood

The zombie roster is one of Dead Island 2’s better ideas.

There are shamblers, runners, brutes, screamers, elemental nightmares and enough mutated freaks to keep fights varied on paper.

Visually, they are excellent.

Mechanically, the results are more mixed.

Some enemies are fun threats. Others start to feel like time taxes. Tankier zombie types can become less scary and more exhausting, especially when you are already trying to reach an objective.

The respawn rate is the bigger issue.

At times, enemies come back so aggressively that fighting stops feeling worthwhile. Instead of enjoying the combat system, I found myself sprinting past encounters because I could not be bothered to clear the same kind of group again.

That is never a great sign.

I was not avoiding enemies because they were terrifying.

I was avoiding them because they were interruptions.

## Story and characters: serviceable, but rarely memorable

Dead Island 2 has the right tone on paper.

It is schlocky, colourful, violent and knowingly ridiculous. That is exactly what a game like this should be.

The issue is that the writing rarely gives you anyone worth caring about.

Most characters blur together. The story moves, but it does not grip. There are jokes that land, but not enough personality to carry the campaign beyond the combat.

There is one standout exception: the underground guy.

He feels like he wandered in from a more interesting version of the game. He has enough weirdness and energy to make you wish the rest of the cast had been pushed further in that direction.

There are also narrative choices that feel oddly handled, including a major beat involving Sam B that should hit harder than it does.

Instead, it lands with a shrug.

By the time the finale arrives, Dead Island 2 feels like it is rushing itself out the door.

Tough fight. Quick wrap. Roll credits.

For a sequel that took so long to arrive, the ending feels strangely underwhelming.

## Sound and vibe: occasionally brilliant

Dead Island 2 deserves credit for its vibe.

The whole game has a trashy, expensive, nightmare-holiday energy that suits it well. It knows it is ridiculous and, most of the time, it has the confidence to enjoy that.

The soundtrack also has some genuinely strong moments.

One boss music sequence in particular captures the exact energy this game needed more of: HELL-A as a nightmare rave, all flashing lights, panic, sweat and undead nonsense.

When Dead Island 2 leans fully into that identity, it works.

I just wish it did it more often.

## Graphics: HELL-A looks gorgeous, and the gore steals the show

Dead Island 2 is a good-looking game, but the gore tech is the real headline.

HELL-A is bright, colourful and full of strong visual contrast. It is not another grey zombie apocalypse, and that alone helps it stand out.

But the FLESH system is what people will remember.

Zombie damage looks detailed, readable and absurdly violent. It gives combat a level of feedback that most zombie games do not have.

You feel every hit because the game makes sure you see every consequence.

It is gross.

It is excessive.

It is easily Dead Island 2’s best feature.

## Gameplay: brilliant melee held back by pacing issues

The melee combat is fantastic when Dead Island 2 gives it room to breathe.

Weapons feel punchy. Environmental kills are satisfying. Co-op makes the chaos better. And the best fights have that perfect “barely under control” feeling.

But the pacing is uneven.

Respawns can become annoying. Tanky enemies can drag fights out. Progression does not always reward experimentation. And exploration restrictions keep pulling you back onto the rails.

The result is a game with excellent moment-to-moment violence and a weaker structure around it.

## Lifespan: short enough to enjoy, not strong enough to obsess over

Dead Island 2 does not massively outstay its welcome, which is a point in its favour.

The campaign is brisk enough that you can play through it, enjoy the carnage and move on before the repetition completely takes over.

But it also did not make me want to mop up everything afterwards.

That is the difference between a fun game and a truly great one.

I finished Dead Island 2 feeling entertained, but not obsessed.

## Who should play Dead Island 2?

You should play Dead Island 2 if you want:

* First-person melee combat that feels meaty and rewarding
* Chaotic co-op with friends
* Top-tier gore that makes every hit look horrific
* A colourful zombie setting with a strong visual identity
* A short, silly campaign that does not demand too much from you

You should skip it, or wait for a big sale, if you want:

* A genuinely strong story and memorable cast
* Deep RPG progression
* Exploration that consistently rewards curiosity
* Enemy pacing that does not push you into sprinting past fights
* A sequel that properly evolves the Dead Island formula

## Final verdict

Dead Island 2 is a gore-soaked melee playground stapled to a story and progression loop that never fully earns your long-term obsession.

When it is good, it is properly good. The combat is crunchy, the gore is ridiculous, and HELL-A gives the series a bright, nasty identity that helps it stand apart from duller zombie games.

But the longer you play, the harder it becomes to ignore the weaker parts.

The story is forgettable. The characters rarely stick. Exploration feels more restricted than it should. Enemy respawns can become maddening. And the loot loop never becomes addictive enough to carry the game beyond its best combat moments.

Dead Island 2 is fun.

It is just not unforgettable.

For co-op chaos with mates, it is an easy recommendation on sale. For series die-hards hoping for a genre-defining sequel, it lands closer to “pretty good” than “finally, they nailed it.”

## FAQ

### Is Dead Island 2 worth playing?

Yes, Dead Island 2 is worth playing if you want brutal first-person melee combat, excellent gore and fun co-op zombie chaos. It is less essential if you care more about story, exploration or deep RPG progression.

### Is Dead Island 2 good solo?

Dead Island 2 is enjoyable solo, especially if you mainly want to enjoy the combat and setting. However, the repetition, enemy respawns and weaker story are more noticeable when you are not playing with friends.

### Is Dead Island 2 better in co-op?

Yes, Dead Island 2 is better in co-op. The campaign supports up to three players, and the game’s chaotic combat, silly tone and messy zombie encounters suit group play well.

### What is the best thing about Dead Island 2?

The best thing about Dead Island 2 is the melee combat, especially the gore system. Every hit feels physical, crunchy and reactive, making zombie fights satisfying even when the wider progression loop feels flat.

### What is the biggest problem with Dead Island 2?

The biggest problem with Dead Island 2 is that its story, progression and exploration do not match the quality of its combat. The game feels brilliant in short bursts, but less compelling over a full campaign.

### Is Dead Island 2 scary?

Dead Island 2 is not especially scary. It is more of a violent, comedic, over-the-top zombie action game than a traditional horror experience.

### How long does Dead Island 2 take to beat?

Dead Island 2 is a relatively brisk campaign by action RPG standards. It is long enough for a satisfying weekend playthrough, but it may not give every player a strong reason to complete all side content afterwards.

### Should I buy Dead Island 2 at full price?

Dead Island 2 is easier to recommend on sale. The combat and co-op are strong, but the weak story, uneven pacing and underwhelming progression make it harder to call an essential full-price purchase.
